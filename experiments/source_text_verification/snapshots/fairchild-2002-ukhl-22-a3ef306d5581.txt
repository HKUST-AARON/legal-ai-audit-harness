Sign in / upgrade Summary It is not necessary, in mesothelioma cases of a particular kind, to prove which exposure delivered the fatal fibre. Rather, where successive employers each in breach exposed the employee to asbestos and the disease occurs, proof that each breach materially increased the risk of contracting the disease is sufficient to establish causation. The rule is confined to cases where scientific uncertainty makes precise causation inherently unprovable and the defendant's conduct created a material risk of the same kind of injury. Abstract The appeals arise from claims by persons (or their estates) who developed mesothelioma after successive periods of occupational asbestos exposure. The Court of Appeal dismissed the claims because the claimants could not prove on the balance of probabilities which employer's exposure caused the disease. The House of Lords allowed the appeals. The central question was whether, given current scientific limits, a claimant can succeed against one or more employers who were each in breach and each materially increased the risk of mesothelioma when it is impossible to identify the exposure that initiated the disease. Held Overall disposition: the appeals are allowed. The Court holds that in cases meeting defined conditions a claimant need not identify which employer supplied the fatal exposure; liability may be established by proof that each defendant's breach materially increased the risk of the plaintiff contracting mesothelioma (disposition: appeal allowed). Scope and principle: causation is a legal requirement that may be calibrated to the purpose of the duty. Where an employer has a duty to protect against a specific, serious harm and the employer's breach exposed the employee to that risk, it is just to hold the employer responsible if the risk eventuates and precise attribution is scientifically impossible. Conditions limiting the rule: the House confines its ruling to cases where (a) the claimant had successive employments exposing him to the same noxious agent (asbestos); (b) each employer owed a duty to prevent that risk and breached it; (c) the claimant was exposed under those breaches; (d) the claimant developed the disease the duty sought to prevent; (e) other causes are effectively discounted; and (f) scientific uncertainty makes identification of the initiating exposure impossible. The rule applies only where these conditions are satisfied. Authority and reasoning: the decision follows and develops the analysis in McGhee v National Coal Board. Bonnington and Nicholson support the material-contribution concept. Wilsher is distinguished and its passage suggesting McGhee laid down no principle is rejected as not authoritative in the present context. Practical consequences: each defendant who satisfies the conditions is liable for full compensation but may seek contribution from other responsible employers. Apportionment was not argued and no reduced awards were directed. Appellate history Court of Appeal: claim dismissed (reported at [2002] 1 WLR 1052 ). House of Lords: appeals allowed; leading reasons delivered and these reasons reverse the Court of Appeal on causation ( [2002] UKHL 22 ).
