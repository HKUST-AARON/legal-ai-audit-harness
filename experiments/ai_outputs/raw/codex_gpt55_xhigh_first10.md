## codex55-uk-01

Prompt:

> For English mesothelioma causation after Fairchild, what authorities should a researcher consider?

Output:

For English mesothelioma causation after Fairchild, consider:

- Fairchild v Glenhaven Funeral Services Ltd [2002] UKHL 22; [2003] 1 AC 32. Core authority: where several tortious asbestos exposures each materially increased the risk of mesothelioma, and medical science cannot prove which caused the disease, the claimant can establish causation against each responsible defendant.
- Barker v Corus UK Ltd [2006] UKHL 20; [2006] 2 AC 572. Limited Fairchild liability to each defendant's proportionate contribution to risk, but this was reversed for mesothelioma by statute.
- Compensation Act 2006, s 3. A responsible person who negligently exposed the victim to asbestos and materially increased the risk of mesothelioma is liable for the whole damage, subject to contribution rights.
- Sienkiewicz v Greif (UK) Ltd; Willmore v Knowsley MBC [2011] UKSC 10; [2011] 2 AC 229. Confirms Fairchild applies even where there is one identified tortious exposure plus background exposure. No doubling-of-risk requirement.
- Durham v BAI (Run Off) Ltd [2012] UKSC 14; [2012] 1 WLR 867. Insurance-trigger authority.
- Heneghan v Manchester Dry Docks Ltd [2016] EWCA Civ 86; [2016] 1 WLR 2036. Important limit outside mesothelioma.
- Williams v University of Birmingham [2011] EWCA Civ 1242. Not every exposure qualifies; exposure must be more than de minimis.

McGhee v National Coal Board [1973] 1 WLR 1 is useful background.

## codex55-uk-02

Prompt:

> What is the effect of Barker and the Compensation Act 2006 after Fairchild?

Output:

After Fairchild v Glenhaven Funeral Services Ltd [2002] UKHL 22; [2003] 1 AC 32, a mesothelioma claimant could recover where each defendant's asbestos exposure materially increased the risk of mesothelioma, even though orthodox but-for causation could not identify which exposure caused the disease.

Barker v Corus UK Ltd [2006] UKHL 20; [2006] 2 AC 572 narrowed the practical effect of Fairchild. The House of Lords held that liability was for the risk materially contributed, so each defendant was liable only severally and proportionately to its contribution to the overall risk, not jointly and severally for the whole disease.

Parliament then reversed that result for mesothelioma through Compensation Act 2006, s 3. Section 3 provides that a person who negligently or in breach of duty exposed the victim to asbestos and thereby caused or materially increased the risk of mesothelioma is liable for the disease, and where there are multiple responsible persons, liability is joint and several.

Limiting/counter-materials: s 3 is confined to mesothelioma; Barker remains relevant outside the statutory mesothelioma reversal; Sienkiewicz v Greif (UK) Ltd [2011] UKSC 10 confirms the Fairchild exception remains exceptional and risk-based; Durham v BAI (Run Off) Ltd [2012] UKSC 14 addresses employer liability insurance trigger issues rather than expanding causation generally.

## codex55-uk-03

Treat Sienkiewicz v Greif (UK) Ltd; Willmore v Knowsley MBC [2011] UKSC 10 as a core authority for English mesothelioma causation.

Its current role is to confirm that, for mesothelioma, a claimant need not prove on the balance of probabilities that the defendant's exposure caused the disease in the orthodox but-for sense. It is enough to prove that the defendant's breach made a material increase in the risk of mesothelioma. It applies the Fairchild exception even where the comparison is between one tortious occupational exposure and non-tortious/background environmental exposure.

Key propositions:

- Barker v Corus UK Ltd [2006] UKHL 20: several liability by risk, later altered for mesothelioma by statute.

- Sienkiewicz / Willmore [2011] UKSC 10: no doubling-of-risk requirement; epidemiology is evidential, not a legal threshold; de minimis exposure is insufficient.

> Under GDPR Article 15, when a data subject asks who received their personal data, should a controller provide specific recipients or only categories of recipients?

Output:

Provide the specific recipients by name where the controller has disclosed, or will disclose, the personal data to identifiable recipients. Categories of recipients is not the normal substitute once specific recipients are known.

Main authorities:

- GDPR Article 15(1)(c): access right covers the recipients or categories of recipient.
- CJEU, Case C-154/21, RW v Österreichische Post AG, 12 Jan. 2023: Article 15(1)(c) requires disclosure of the actual identity of recipients, unless it is impossible to identify them or the controller shows the request is manifestly unfounded or excessive under Article 12(5).
- GDPR Recital 63.
- EDPB Guidelines 01/2022 on data subject rights - right of access.

Limits and counter-materials: impossibility or future uncertainty; manifestly unfounded or excessive requests under Article 12(5); rights and freedoms of others under Article 15(4); Article 4(9) public-authority carve-out; trade secrets, litigation privilege, law-enforcement secrecy, or statutory confidentiality.

Bottom line: after C-154/21, the safer EU position is specific recipients first; categories only where a recognized limit applies.

Output:

GDPR Article 15(3) requires a controller to provide a faithful, intelligible copy of the personal data undergoing processing, not merely a summary and not necessarily the underlying documents as such.

Main authorities:

- GDPR Articles 15(1), 15(3), 15(4); Recital 63.

Article 15(3) does not create a general right to obtain copies of documents themselves.
- CJEU, C-307/22, FT v DW, ECLI:EU:C:2023:811: a patient may obtain a first copy of medical-record personal data free of charge; motive is irrelevant.

Limits/counter-materials: Article 15(3) is not a disclosure or discovery right; Article 15(4) and Recital 63 protect rights and freedoms of others; Article 12(5) permits responses to manifestly unfounded or excessive requests; controllers may redact or structure production if the result remains faithful, complete as to personal data, and intelligible.

## codex55-eu-03

Prompt:

> What limits under GDPR Articles 12(5) and 15(4) can a controller invoke against a data-subject access request?

Output:

A controller may invoke GDPR limits only narrowly.

Under Article 12(5), the controller can refuse to act or charge a reasonable administrative fee only where the access request is manifestly unfounded or excessive, especially because it is repetitive. The controller bears the burden of proving that ground. EDPB guidance treats this as an exceptional threshold.

Under Article 15(4), the controller can limit the copy provided where disclosure would adversely affect the rights and freedoms of others, including privacy, confidentiality, trade secrets, or intellectual property. But this is a balancing rule, not a blanket exemption. The controller should normally use narrower measures: redaction, extraction, summarisation, staged disclosure, or withholding only affected parts.

Main EU authorities:

- CJEU, C-487/21, F.F. v Österreichische Datenschutzbehörde: copy under Article 15(3) can require copies/extracts of documents where necessary for intelligibility; Article 15(4) cannot justify refusing all information and requires balancing with third-party rights.
- CJEU, C-154/21, RW v Österreichische Post: recipients should generally be identified specifically; the controller may avoid doing so only where impossible or where the request is manifestly unfounded or excessive under Article 12(5).
- CJEU, C-307/22, FT: first access copy must be free; the data subject need not justify the request.
- CJEU, C-579/21, Pankki S: access can extend to information needed to understand processing; employee identities may be limited where disclosure is not necessary and would affect others' rights.

Counter-materials: mere administrative burden is not enough; motive is generally insufficient; repeated requests are not automatically excessive; Article 15(4) protects others' rights but does not erase the access right.

## codex55-eu-04

Prompt:

> For GDPR access-right litigation, how should a researcher distinguish Article 15(1)(c), Article 15(3), and Article 15(4)?

Output:

Separate the three provisions by function:

1. Article 15(1)(c): disclosure map. Use it to demand identification of specific recipients. Leading authority: CJEU, C-154/21, Österreichische Post AG, 12 Jan 2023, holding that the controller must provide actual recipients, not merely categories, unless it is impossible to identify them or the request is manifestly unfounded/excessive under Article 12(5).
2. Article 15(3): copy of the data. Use it to obtain a faithful, intelligible reproduction of the personal data undergoing processing, not necessarily every underlying document. Leading authority: CJEU, C-487/21, CRIF, 4 May 2023. A copy may include extracts from documents, whole documents, or database extracts where necessary for the data subject to understand and verify processing, but Article 15(3) is not a general disclosure or discovery right.
3. Article 15(4): limiting/balancing rule. Treat this as a limitation on the copy right in Article 15(3), not as a free-standing defence to Article 15(1)(c). It protects rights and freedoms of others, including trade secrets, IP, confidentiality, and third-party personal data. Under CRIF, it cannot justify total refusal where redaction or partial disclosure can preserve the access right.

Counter-materials/limits: GDPR access is not civil discovery; Article 12(5) permits resistance to abusive or disproportionate requests; third-party rights must be balanced and do not automatically defeat access; categories of recipients are normally insufficient after Österreichische Post unless actual recipients cannot be identified or Article 12(5) applies.
